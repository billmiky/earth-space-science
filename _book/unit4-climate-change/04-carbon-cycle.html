17.8 3.6 Solar Cycles: Does the Sun’s Output Change?

17.8.1 A New Question

The Milankovitch theory explains climate changes based on how Earth’s position affects the distribution of solar radiation across the planet. But what if the Sun itself changes—what if it produces more or less energy over time? Could variations in the Sun’s energy output also contribute to climate change?

The Sun is not a perfectly constant star. Its energy output varies slightly over time, driven by changes in solar magnetic activity. The most visible evidence of this is sunspots—dark regions on the Sun’s surface that indicate strong magnetic activity.

17.10.3 Driving Question Answered

What factors have contributed to climate change in the past and are they contributing to climate change now?

Milankovitch cycles (changes in Earth’s orbit and axial tilt) have been the primary driver of major climate changes over the past several hundred thousand years, causing glacial-interglacial cycles through changes in high-latitude summer insolation. However, current orbital parameters predict gradual cooling, not the rapid warming observed over the past 200 years. This discrepancy indicates that orbital factors are NOT responsible for current climate change—other factors must be at play. In the next chapter, you’ll investigate what those factors might be.

## 3.2 Milankovitch Cycles: Earth's Orbital Variations

### Introduction to Orbital Factors

In the 1920s, Serbian scientist Milutin Milanković proposed that long-term changes in Earth's orbit and axial tilt could explain the glacial-interglacial cycles[1]. These variations, now called Milankovitch cycles, affect how much solar radiation reaches different parts of Earth at different times of year.

There are three main orbital factors that change over time:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Eccentricity} - The shape of Earth's orbit around the Sun
\item \textbf{Obliquity (Tilt)} - The angle of Earth's rotational axis
\item \textbf{Precession} - The direction Earth's axis points in space
\end{enumerate}

### 3.2.1 Eccentricity: The Shape of Earth's Orbit

**What is it?**

Eccentricity describes how circular or elliptical (oval-shaped) Earth's orbit is. When eccentricity is low, Earth's orbit is nearly circular. When eccentricity is high, the orbit is more elliptical, meaning Earth gets closer to and farther from the Sun during its yearly revolution.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\caption{Earth's orbital eccentricity varies between nearly circular (low eccentricity) and more elliptical (high eccentricity) over approximately 100,000-year cycles.}
\label{fig:eccentricity}
\end{figure}

**Interactive Visualization:** Manipulate Earth's orbital eccentricity and observe how it changes Earth's distance from the Sun: [Earth's Orbit Simulator - University of Nebraska-Lincoln](https://astro.unl.edu/classaction/animations/coordsmotion/ecliptic.html)

**Cycle length:** Approximately 100,000 years (with variations around 95,000-125,000 years)

**How does it affect climate?**

\begin{itemize}
\item When Earth's orbit is more elliptical, there is a greater difference between the closest approach (perihelion) and farthest distance (aphelion) from the Sun
\item This affects the total amount of solar radiation Earth receives throughout the year
\item The effect is relatively small compared to other orbital factors (only about 0.2\% variation in solar energy)
\end{itemize}

**Current status:** Earth's orbit is currently nearly circular (low eccentricity) and becoming more circular over the next 50,000 years.

### 3.2.2 Obliquity: Earth's Axial Tilt

**What is it?**

Obliquity is the angle between Earth's rotational axis and a line perpendicular to its orbital plane. You learned in Chapter 2 that this tilt is what causes seasons. However, the amount of tilt changes over time.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\caption{Earth's axial tilt (obliquity) varies between 22.1° and 24.5° over approximately 41,000-year cycles. Current tilt is 23.4° and decreasing.}
\label{fig:obliquity}
\end{figure>

**Interactive Demo:** Adjust Earth's axial tilt and observe how it affects seasonal sunlight distribution: [Seasons Interactive - University of Nebraska-Lincoln](https://astro.unl.edu/classaction/animations/coordsmotion/obliquity.html)

**Cycle length:** Approximately 41,000 years

**How does it affect climate?**

\begin{itemize}
\item Greater tilt means more extreme seasons - hotter summers and colder winters, especially at high latitudes
\item Lesser tilt means milder seasons - cooler summers and warmer winters
\item Cool summers are critical for ice sheet growth because snow that falls in winter doesn't fully melt in summer
\item High-latitude summer temperatures are the key factor determining whether ice sheets grow or shrink
\end{itemize}

**Current status:** Earth's tilt is currently 23.4° and decreasing (moving toward less extreme seasons).

### 3.2.3 Precession: The Direction of Earth's Tilt

**What is it?**

Precession refers to the slow "wobble" of Earth's axis, similar to how a spinning top wobbles. This changes which direction Earth's North Pole points in space. Currently, Earth's axis points toward Polaris (the North Star), but 13,000 years ago it pointed toward the star Vega.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\caption{Earth's axis precesses (wobbles) over approximately 26,000-year cycles, changing the direction the North Pole points in space and altering when perihelion and aphelion occur during the year.}
\label{fig:precession}
\end{figure}

**Cycle length:** Approximately 26,000 years (combined with orbital precession creates a 21,000-year cycle)

**How does it affect climate?**

Precession determines the timing of when Earth is closest to and farthest from the Sun relative to the seasons:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Currently:} Northern Hemisphere summer occurs when Earth is farthest from the Sun (aphelion), making summers slightly cooler
\item \textbf{~11,000 years ago:} Northern Hemisphere summer occurred when Earth was closest to the Sun (perihelion), making summers warmer
\item This affects how intense the seasons are in each hemisphere
\item Warmer summers at high northern latitudes can melt more ice, preventing ice sheet growth
\end{itemize}

**Current status:** Northern Hemisphere is currently tilted away from the Sun during perihelion (closest approach), resulting in relatively cool summers.

### Combining the Three Factors

The three Milankovitch cycles operate simultaneously and combine to influence climate:

\begin{itemize}
\item The 100,000-year eccentricity cycle appears to pace the major glacial-interglacial transitions
\item The 41,000-year tilt cycle and 21,000-year precession cycle create shorter-term variations
\item When these cycles align to produce cool northern hemisphere summers, ice sheets can grow, leading to glacial periods
\item When they align to produce warm northern hemisphere summers, ice sheets melt, leading to interglacial periods
\end{itemize}

**Key Concept:** High-latitude summer insolation is the critical factor. Cool summers allow snow and ice to persist year-round, enabling ice sheets to grow. Warm summers cause melting that prevents or reverses ice sheet growth.

### Synthesis: The Milankovitch Theory

After analyzing these three major climate transitions, you should be able to see a pattern:

\begin{itemize}
\item Glacial-to-interglacial transitions coincide with orbital configurations that increase summer radiation at high northern latitudes
\item Interglacial-to-glacial transitions coincide with orbital configurations that decrease summer radiation at high northern latitudes

\end{itemize}

**Key Insight:** Ice sheets grow when high-latitude summers are cool (snow doesn't melt), and they shrink when high-latitude summers are warm (snow and ice melt). Milankovitch cycles change the intensity of high-latitude summers, thereby controlling ice sheet growth and retreat.

## 3.6 Solar Cycles: Does the Sun's Output Change?

### A New Question

The Milankovitch theory explains climate changes based on how Earth's position affects the distribution of solar radiation across the planet. But what if the Sun itself changes—what if it produces more or less energy over time? Could variations in the Sun's energy output also contribute to climate change?

### Understanding Solar Cycles

The Sun is not a perfectly constant star. Its energy output varies slightly over time, driven by changes in solar magnetic activity. The most visible evidence of this is **sunspots**—dark regions on the Sun's surface that indicate strong magnetic activity.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\caption{Sunspot activity from 2002 (solar maximum) to 2010 (solar minimum), showing changes in the number of sunspots and corresponding ultraviolet radiation output. Brighter images indicate more solar radiation.}
\label{fig:sunspots}
\end{figure}

**Interactive Data Explorer:** Explore current and historical sunspot activity with real NASA data: [NOAA Solar Cycle Progression](https://www.swpc.noaa.gov/products/solar-cycle-progression)

**Real-Time Solar Activity:** View the Sun right now with live satellite imagery: [NASA Solar Dynamics Observatory](https://sdo.gsfc.nasa.gov/data/)

**Key observations:**

\begin{itemize}
\item More sunspots = higher solar radiation output
\item Fewer sunspots = lower solar radiation output
\item The Sun goes through regular cycles of high and low activity
\end{itemize}

\end{enumerate}

### Key Findings About Solar Cycles

Based on your analysis:

\begin{itemize}
\item Solar cycles last approximately **11 years**
\item Glacial-interglacial cycles last approximately **100,000 years**
\item The timescales don't match—solar cycles are far too short to explain ice age cycles
\item Solar radiation does vary slightly, and this can influence climate on short timescales (decades to centuries)
\item However, solar variations cannot explain the major glacial-interglacial transitions
\end{itemize}

**Conclusion:** While the Sun's output does vary over time, these variations operate on a much shorter timescale than the major climate changes of the past 400,000 years. Milankovitch cycles, not solar cycles, provide the best explanation for glacial-interglacial cycling.

## 3.8 Chapter Summary

### Key Concepts

\begin{itemize}
\item Earth has experienced regular glacial-interglacial cycles over the past 400,000+ years, with ice ages occurring approximately every 100,000 years
\item Milankovitch cycles—variations in Earth's orbital eccentricity (100,000-year cycle), axial tilt (41,000-year cycle), and precession (21,000-year cycle)—change the amount and distribution of solar radiation reaching Earth's surface
\item High-latitude summer insolation is the critical factor controlling ice sheet growth and retreat: cool summers allow ice sheets to grow, warm summers cause them to melt
\item Physical and computational models demonstrate a strong correlation between Milankovitch cycles and glacial-interglacial temperature changes
\item A causal mechanism exists: orbital configuration → changes in insolation → changes in ice sheet extent → changes in global temperature
\item Solar output varies on an 11-year cycle but cannot explain long-term glacial-interglacial cycles due to timescale mismatch
\item Current orbital parameters predict Earth should be cooling gradually, yet temperatures have risen rapidly over the past 200 years, indicating that factors other than orbital variations must be driving current climate change
\end{itemize}

### Three-Dimensional Learning

**Science and Engineering Practices:**

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Developing and Using Models:} You used physical models to investigate how orbital factors affect insolation and computational models to test the Milankovitch theory against real data
\item \textbf{Analyzing and Interpreting Data:} You analyzed temperature and insolation data spanning 400,000 years, identifying patterns and relationships
\item \textbf{Constructing Explanations:} You developed mechanistic explanations for how orbital variations cause climate changes
\end{itemize}

**Crosscutting Concepts:**

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Patterns:} Recognizing cyclical patterns in temperature data and orbital parameters
\item \textbf{Cause and Effect:} Establishing causal relationships between orbital variations and climate change through mechanism, timing, and consistency
\item \textbf{Stability and Change:} Understanding that climate systems have natural variability but can be disrupted by factors operating outside normal ranges
\item \textbf{Scale, Proportion, and Quantity:} Comparing timescales of different climate forcing factors (11-year solar cycles vs. 100,000-year ice age cycles vs. 200-year industrial warming)
\end{itemize}

**Disciplinary Core Ideas:**

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ESS1.B Earth and the Solar System:} Cyclical changes in Earth's orbital parameters affect climate over tens to hundreds of thousands of years
\item \textbf{ESS2.A Earth Materials and Systems:} Ice sheets respond to changes in energy input, creating major changes in Earth systems
\item \textbf{ESS2.D Weather and Climate:} Climate varies naturally on multiple timescales due to different forcing mechanisms
\end{itemize}

### Driving Question Answered

**What factors have contributed to climate change in the past and are they contributing to climate change now?**

Milankovitch cycles (changes in Earth's orbit and axial tilt) have been the primary driver of major climate changes over the past several hundred thousand years, causing glacial-interglacial cycles through changes in high-latitude summer insolation. However, current orbital parameters predict gradual cooling, not the rapid warming observed over the past 200 years. This discrepancy indicates that orbital factors are NOT responsible for current climate change—other factors must be at play. In the next chapter, you'll investigate what those factors might be.

### Looking Ahead

You've ruled out natural orbital variations as the cause of recent warming. You've also seen that solar output variations operate on too short a timescale. This leads to a critical question for the next chapter:

**If natural factors can't explain current warming, what can?**

In Chapter 4, you'll investigate the role of greenhouse gases, feedback mechanisms, and human activities in driving rapid climate change.
